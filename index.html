Search › Supplements › Berberine
Supplement

Berberine

Taken for blood sugar, cholesterol, and (often) weight loss.

Overall evidence: Mixed

It nudges blood sugar and cholesterol a little — but it isn’t Ozempic, and it isn’t a weight-loss shortcut.

What the evidence shows

ModerateBlood sugar: small trials show modest drops in fasting glucose and HbA1c — real, but weaker and shorter-lived than metformin.
ModerateCholesterol: small reductions in LDL and triglycerides across several studies.
WeakWeight loss: minimal and inconsistent — not a meaningful weight-loss tool.

Safety & interactions

  • Commonly causes stomach upset, cramping, or diarrhea, especially at higher doses.
  • Interacts with several prescription medicines (including metformin, blood thinners, and some blood-pressure and immune drugs) — it can change how they work.
  • Not recommended in pregnancy or breastfeeding.

Who might consider it

People with mildly elevated blood sugar or cholesterol who want to add something — with their doctor’s okay, and never in place of proven treatment.

What to do

  • Reasonable to try for blood sugar or cholesterol support if your doctor agrees — alongside, not instead of, proven care.
  • Keep expectations modest, and stop if it upsets your stomach.
  • Never use it to replace a prescribed medication like metformin or a statin.
How to talk to your doctor: “I’m considering berberine for my blood sugar — does it interact with anything I take, and is it reasonable alongside my current plan?”

Search › Claims & trends › Seed oils
Claim · Myth-check

Do seed oils cause inflammation?

Verdict: Mostly a myth

For most people, in normal amounts, seed oils (canola, soybean, sunflower) don’t drive inflammation. The “toxic seed oil” panic runs well ahead of the evidence.

What we actually know

StrongIn trials, replacing saturated fat with these oils tends to lower inflammation markers and improve cholesterol.
ModerateThe real problem is ultra-processed food (often fried in these oils) — that’s the junk, not the oil itself.
WeakClaims that seed oils are uniquely “toxic” or a main driver of chronic disease aren’t supported.

What to do

  • No need to fear seed oils in normal home cooking.
  • The bigger lever for health is less ultra-processed food and more whole foods — regardless of the oil.
  • Olive oil is a great everyday choice too; variety is fine.
How to raise it with your doctor: “I keep seeing that seed oils are dangerous — does my diet actually need changing, or is this overblown for me?”

Condition · Diagnosis

GERD (acid reflux)

Stomach acid repeatedly backs up into your esophagus, causing heartburn and regurgitation. Very common and very treatable — and rarely dangerous, though persistent symptoms deserve a proper check.

What it is

The valve between your stomach and esophagus relaxes when it shouldn’t, letting acid rise. Typical signs: burning behind the breastbone (often after meals or lying down), a sour taste, or a recurrent cough or hoarse voice.

What we do about it

  • Everyday steps: smaller meals, not lying down for 2–3 hours after eating, raising the head of the bed, easing back on late meals, alcohol, and personal triggers.
  • Medicines, stepped up as needed: antacids → H2 blockers (famotidine) → proton-pump inhibitors (PPIs, e.g. omeprazole) for frequent symptoms.

What the evidence shows

StrongPPIs are highly effective for frequent reflux and for healing the esophagus.
ModerateWeight loss and not eating late genuinely reduce symptoms for many people.
WeakMost popular “reflux diets” and supplements have little solid evidence beyond the basics.

What a doctor typically does

Most cases are diagnosed from your symptoms and a trial of treatment — no test needed. A doctor considers an endoscopy if symptoms persist despite treatment, start later in life, or come with warning signs.

See a doctor promptly if you have: trouble or pain swallowing, unintentional weight loss, vomiting blood or black stools, or symptoms that won’t settle with treatment.
How to talk to your doctor: track when it happens and what triggers it, then ask: “Is this GERD or something else, should I try a PPI, and how long before we look closer?”

Medication

Ozempic (semaglutide)

A once-weekly injection for type 2 diabetes — and, as Wegovy, for weight loss.

A genuinely powerful drug for blood sugar and weight — with real side effects, a high price, and weight that tends to return if you stop. Not a casual quick fix.

What it’s for & how it works

It’s a GLP-1 medication: it slows digestion, blunts appetite, and helps the body manage blood sugar. Prescribed for type 2 diabetes (Ozempic) and, at higher doses, for obesity (Wegovy).

What to expect

Meaningful, gradual results over months — often around 10–15% body-weight loss on the weight-loss dose — but it works while you take it, not after.

How well it works

StrongBlood sugar & weight: large trials show substantial, reliable reductions.
StrongHeart: lowers cardiovascular events in higher-risk patients.

Side effects & cautions

  • Nausea, vomiting, and other gut symptoms are common, especially while the dose is increasing.
  • Rare but serious risks include pancreatitis and gallbladder problems; not used in pregnancy.
  • Weight and blood-sugar gains often return after stopping; muscle loss can occur if you under-eat.

Who it’s for / not for

For people with type 2 diabetes, or weight in a range where it improves health — under medical supervision. It’s not a cosmetic quick fix, and it isn’t right for everyone.

How to talk to your doctor: “Am I a good candidate, what side effects should I watch for, what does it cost with my coverage, and what’s the long-term plan?”

Search › Tests & screening › Full-body MRI
Test · Screening

Full-body MRI scans

Marketed scans that image you head-to-pelvis to “catch problems early.”

For most healthy people, not worth it. They find harmless oddities far more often than dangerous disease — leading to anxiety, more tests, and cost.

What it is

A head-to-pelvis MRI looking for things like tumours or aneurysms — usually sold direct-to-consumer, without a doctor’s referral.

When it’s worth it — and who should skip

Most symptom-free people should skip it. It can occasionally make sense for specific high-risk situations your doctor identifies — but not as a routine “just in case.”

What results really mean

CommonIncidental findings: harmless quirks turn up often — and each can trigger more scans, biopsies, and worry.
No benefitThere’s no good evidence these scans help healthy people live longer.
How to talk to your doctor: “I saw an ad for a full-body scan — is it useful for me, or should I stick to the screenings recommended for my age and risk?”

Search › Symptoms › Headache
Symptom

Headache

Most headaches are tension or migraine and aren’t dangerous — but a few specific patterns need urgent care.

Get care now if your headache is…

  • The sudden “worst headache of your life,” or it peaks within seconds to minutes.
  • With fever and a stiff neck, confusion, fainting, weakness or numbness, or trouble speaking or seeing.
  • After a head injury — or new and persistent if you’re over 50 or have a cancer history.

What usually causes it

Most often tension-type (tight, band-like) or migraine (throbbing, with light/sound sensitivity and sometimes nausea). Dehydration, poor sleep, screens, caffeine changes, and overusing painkillers are common triggers.

What to do at home

  • Water, rest, and a little caffeine can help — and treating early works best.
  • Use over-the-counter pain relief sparingly; taking it more than ~2 days a week can cause “rebound” headaches.
  • Track timing and triggers to spot a pattern.

When to see a doctor (non-urgent)

If headaches are frequent, getting worse, needing regular painkillers, or changing in pattern — it’s worth a visit to pin down the type and a plan.

How to talk to your doctor: bring a short log — when they hit, how long, what helped — and ask: “What type is this, and what’s the plan to prevent them?”
